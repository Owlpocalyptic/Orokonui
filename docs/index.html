Project Overview

Professional

Back to top

This project is a multidisciplinary team effort created as part of the "Games" department of Project 1. Students working on this project will be and have been working with two students of Otago Polytechnic's Design school, for our mutual client Orokonui Ecosanctuary. This client's core concern is the conservation of South Island k훮k훮, a native species of parrot that has recently been brought back from extinction in the Otago region. More specifically, their goal for this partnership is to have an educational tool that will get people in Dunedin and Otago engaged in their conservation efforts, and informed about how best to help k훮k훮 regain a footing in this area of the country.

So far, we've created two main "bands" of work through which we can accomplish these goals. The first is a GPS-based k훮k훮 reporting tool, through which conscientious citizens can report sightings of k훮k훮s to Orokonui itself. The tool provides a reason for people with a passing interest in k훮k훮 to regularly think about k훮k훮, and a way to help Orokonui Ecosanctuary gather data about k훮k훮 behaviour as they grow increasingly less reliant on the protection that the Ecosanctuary provides them. This tool is built on Unity, and may one day be used to track k훮k훮 movement live via GPS tracker bands attached to them.

The other, more complete product is a website. This is more directly educational than the mobile app, and uses a narrative-based approach to teach visitors about ways that their actions can indirectly help (or harm) k훮k훮s. There will also be other resources available on this site, such as links to Orokonui's own documentation on techniques and best practices to help make neighbourhoods safer for k훮k훮 visitors, and the Halo Project (largely responsible for the eradication of invasive predator species known to prey on native birds in Orokonui's vicinity).

The more discerning among you may notice, as you read the earlier entries on this site, that Orokonui is not mentioned at all until you get some way into the first half of this semester. This is largely because for the first few weeks of our project, Orokonui was but a whisper of a rumour of a mystery, and I was working towards a VR experience for an advocate for the visually disabled. Eventually our supervisor decided to split us up into three separate nominally games-related projects, and it was at this point that Orokonui became my client.

Finishing The Tutorial

28/2/2019

Technical

Back to top

Egads and hurrah! I have finished the tutorial, and begun to implement some novel features. One such feature is severely reducing the player's mobility if they're ever in the air, to reflect how in real life being airborne makes it difficult to, for example, reverse the direction of your momentum and fly back on to a ramp that you've driven off. I did this by using the "tag" system and the OnCollisionExit and OnCollisionStay events native to Unity: the final solution was much simpler than I expected it to be, which is great.

I also started exporting builds of this game, both to my Windows machine and my Android phone. The text on the Android builds so far has been somewhat unpredictable: I hope to find some way to preview UIs as they will show up on Android devices, but this is made somewhat difficult by the range of dimensions that these devices can have.

New Objectives

4/3/2019

Design

I tragically forgot my phone cable today, so I spent most of this session changing the goal of the Roll A Ball game completely. Instead of collecting 12 yellow cubes without any failure condition, I transformed the playing field into a sort of maze that the player has to navigate in order to win. But this maze is not without its hazards-- it features a bottomless pit, that will cause the player to lose if they foolishly roll their ball into it.

The reason for this change is to make the game more believably challenging to execute with impaired vision: by introducing a loss condition, the game more accurately reflects the risks that the visually impaired often have to take when doing things that the sighted often consider perfectly safe.

Out Of Control

18/3/2019

Technical

Back to top

Today I tried to enhance my control scheme by moving the camera as the ball moves through the space. My main goal was to give the player the freedom to make choices at intersections and the ability to reverse their direction, but otherwise keep them from running the ball into a position where no movement is possible (unless it's an "official" trap that will swiftly end the game for them).

This... didn't really work. I kept on running into issues with defining areas where the camera was supposed to switch directions. Initially I thought to use Unity's native tag system as a solution, the idea being that I could tag stretches of floor where I wanted the camera to point North, and stretches where it should point South, and so on. Unfortunately this caused lots of issues with the boundaries between stretches of floor (the camera kept rapidly flickering between the two directions, and this prevented the player from making much progress), and didn't solve the key issue of the player pinning themselves against walls.

Then I tried a more programmatic approach, the basic idea being that the camera's direction would be moved based on the direction of the ball's movement. This was a little more successful, at least as far as preventing the flickering bug of the last strategy, but once again kept on pinning the player. It was also at this point that I unwittingly introduced a problematic bug that only let the ball go East or South, effectively reducing the player's range of movement to a single 90 degree slice.

Stuck And Ready To

21/3/2019

Technical

Back to top

I gave the VR control scheme/camera control for my Roll A Ball another go today. This time, my great brainwave was to combine one of my previous, unsuccessful control schemes with the Google Cardboard's single touch input: in addition to moving based on the camera's direction, the ball would only be able to move while the touch input was pressed down. In theory, this would allow the player to "pause" the ball and change their direction at their leisure, allowing them to escape from the dead-end traps that have plagued me for the last week and making the ball less suicidally responsive in the same stroke.

Unfortunately, things did not shake out this way. Stopping and starting the ball did not really address the pinning issue: in all of my tests, I was unable to use it to avoid trapping myself before I even reached the first loss condition, much less the finish line.

After figuring this out, I started looking at rotating the camera at the player's discretion, but couldn't figure out a satisfactory way to have both this input and the input for letting the player actually move.

Shifting Perspective

28/3/2019

Technical

Back to top

I played around with a camera object in a fresh Unity scene today. My goal was to produce something similar to the camera in the evolution sandbox game Spore, so that I could better understand how the Quaternion objects used by Unity work. The way this camera works is that it can be clicked and dragged around a fixed point in the centre of the screen, so that the player can view their creation from all angles (making it easier to edit). It can't be dragged so that it's beneath the floor, or directly above the creation so that it looks directly down on its centre.

My starting point for this control scheme was the "Input.GetAxis()" method native to Unity, which (as you might have intuited) returns a 3D vector based on the mouse's current position. It can take a string as an argument, allowing developers to split the vector returned into X and Y components. This seemed to be the right port of call for my intentions, as I figured it could be used to somehow translate the movement of the mouse into movement of the camera object.

The method for this translation seemed to be "Transform.RotateAround()" method, which takes three arguments-- the vector representing the point to rotate around, the vector representing the line around which the rotation should take place, and a float representing the angle of the rotation. This last float seemed like the proper place to insert the float associated with the magnitude of the mouse's movement, but in practice this produced some strange behaviour once I moved the mouse in a combination of directions.

Intermission: Security Seminar

2/4/2019

Professional

Back to top

Today I attended a seminar on security in an Information Technology context. The speaker, Otago's own Thomas [lastname], raised a number of points with the underlying theme of "The IT industry often forgets that the minimum requirement for security is safety". While some of the analogies he used and conclusions he reached didn't seem completely watertight, overall I do think his case was strong, and definitely worth thinking about.

I did think that one form of harm did go unrepresented here: Thomas covered a category that I'll refer to as "psychological assault", encompassing both shock videos like the livestream of the recent Christchurch shooting and targeted harassment from real-life contacts (an example being an abusive ex) intended to threaten and unsettle their recepients. This is of course a very important facet of the discussion at hand here, but I'm also wondering about the potential for bad actors using the same systems to spread misinformation. A great deal of harmful pseudoscience has sprung off the platforms provided by social media giants like Facebook and Youtube, and it's starting to have repercussions offline, like the revival of various easily vaccinated-against diseases by the "anti-vax" movement. One of the chief culprits seems to be these applications' machine learning algorithms: though they were created to serve a relatively innocuous purpose (helping users find the content most similar to the content they like best), this can produce dangerous results when that content is, itself, dangerous.

While this might seem like more of a philosophical or even legal problem than a strictly technical one, it is a problem that has ramifications on the world of information technology-- what the lawmakers eventually decide is appropriate to create will inevitably shape what we are allowed to create, in much the same way that architects are no longer allowed to design buildings without adequate fire escape routes. It seems counterproductive to cite our own inexperience and cut ourselves out of the discussion entirely.

Today I was finally able to sit down with our clients from Orokonui and make some headway in understanding the exact qualities of the application I'm supposed to be creating. The main technical takeaway is that the application should be quite accessible: a solution that only works at Orokonui (like ones that involve Hololens or similar VR technology) could be great and immersive, but it'll only work on people who are already willing to go to Orokonui. The core goal was given as "engaging the local community in K훮k훮 conservation efforts", and we were able to come up with several possible implementations. The most exciting one, an app that shows in real time where tagged K훮k훮s currently are in the Dunedin area, is also the least likely one to come to fruition: our contacts at Orokonui said that the earliest we can expect any headway on that technology is August, making it a less-than-ideal starting point. Another, more education-oriented idea we had was creating a sort of interactive story, in which players must guide a k훮k훮 or family of k훮k훮s to survival. The idea is that this application would be able to teach people without seeming like it's preaching to them, by letting them discover for themselves what kind of things hurt k훮k훮s in the wild. Fun With JSON

27/5/2019

Technical

Back to top

Thanks to some insights from our glorious supervisor Adon, I was able to produce a very scaleable interactive game in Javascript for the Orokonui K훮k훮 web app. The "trick" is that it uses a single JSON file to store all the information about each scenario: the prompt, the name of the associated image file, and most importantly the IDs of the scenarios each choice in the scenario leads to. When I first had the idea of using a JSON object to store all of this data, I had trouble with actually extracting the data from the object. Initially I had every page stored in an array of JSON objects, each with its own unique ID value, but this made extracting an individual page overcomplicated: I would have had to query the whole array in search of a given ID, and then return all the values associated with that array. Adon suggested the simpler approach of using each page's ID as a key in an overarching JSON object, with every other aspect of the page going into the value tied to that key.

OLD WAY

{
"pages": [
{
"id" : "1a",
"choices": [ "Look around at the trees", "Look around at the animals" ],
"prompt" : "...",
"choice-ids": [ "2a", "2b" ]
}, ...
]
}

NEW WAY

{
"pages":{
"1a" : {
"choices": [ "Look around at the trees", "Look around at the animals" ],
"prompt" : "...",
"choice-ids": [ "2a", "2b" ]
}, ...
}
}

This worked a lot more cleanly, especially when it came to passing ID strings to the prototype I put in charge of cutting relevant information out of the JSON file.

Quality Of Life And Bug Fixes

20/6/2019

Technical

Design

Back to top

Following the feedback our client gave us last week, I implemented a "go back" button that appears after every loss, which allows players to immediately undo their game-losing mistake and keep going. In the process of implementing this feature, I came across the cause of a minor bug. The symptom of the bug was that as a player selected choices, "flashes" of previous choices would consistently start appearing before the result of the player's current choice appeared, as if the game were loading those choices first. Using my galaxy-sized brain and towering, Rick-and-Morty-watching intellect, I realized that this was exactly what was happening: each time my code loaded a new scene, it attached a "mousedown" event to each choice button via jQuery without removing the mousedown events attached in the previous screens. For the earlier choices, this didn't have much of an effect, as the page was able to run down the list of mousedown events very quickly, but as more and more events were added to the stack this phenomenon slowed down the page considerably.

Fortunately, once I figured it out it was relatively easy to google jQuery ways to remove listeners from existing input elements. I was briefly confused between "onclick" and "mousedown" events, but when I learned how to read my own code I soon found this handy method. Now my game calls it on each button before it tries to add any new mousedown listeners to them, resulting in expected behaviour.

Seeing Be... Leaving

17/10/2019

Technical

Design

Back to top

Yesterday, the design crew asked about revamping the movement scheme for the VR game. Instead of free teleportation, they want to experiment with allowing players to teleport to specific points: and instead of using a controller to do so, they want it to be based on line-of-sight, so that all a player has to do is look at an orb and then, after a short delay during which they can abort the teleportation, teleport to that orb.

The initial set of orbs.

I made it work. To get the delayed behaviour, as well as display a rapidly shrinking circle that acts as feedback for the player (so that they know they are going to teleport), I used a coroutine, which starts when the player's line of sight crosses a teleportation orb's collider (about 3x larger than the orbs are visually) and ends either when the teleportation successfully completes, or when the coroutine is aborted by the player breaking line of sight with the collider.

The teleportation coroutine.

This code is actually mostly derived from the "look to highlight" code I did in Highlight Reel.

This was mostly straightforward-- the few issues I ran into were silly mistakes with forgetting to assign values to certain variables, and at one point accidentally assigning to a captured copy of an object rather than the object itself. Once I got the system into a working state, I did make one key quality of life change: because teleporting to an orb put you within that orb's collider (since, as I mentioned earlier, the collider of an orb is significantly larger than the orb itself), it was somewhat difficult to get a lock on a second orb after teleporting once.

As a player is unlikely to want to teleport to the orb that they just teleported to (since they're already there), I solved this issue by making the teleport method a) capture the target orb's details after successfully teleporting to an orb, then b) deactivate that orb until the next time a teleportation is successfully triggered. This way, teleporting to an orb temporarily removes it from the game world, until you teleport away (restoring the first orb and temporarily removing the second one). This elegantly removes the collider problem, and stops the player from accidentally triggering mini-teleports while trying to look down.

Quality Of Life

Today was mostly focused on fixing the minor, quibbling issues that made the game feel more buggy than it needed to. Chief among these was fixing the behaviour of corpses: now, instead of just vanishing some time after a bird dies, it fades out of existence with relative grace. This was achieved through judicious application of shaders-- in life, each k훮k훮 uses the "Legacy/Transparent/Cutout/Bumped Diffuse" shader, and in death, they use the "Unlit/UnlitAlphaWithFade" shader. I made this determination because although the Unlit shader allows me to fade out the model's mesh render gracefully, its base state is also fairly transparent, and makes the k훮k훮 model look really ugly and surreal (since you can see its legs through its back, which is not normally how k훮k훮 anatomy works).

The other major issue I fixed was to do with k훮k훮 pathfinding towards bread: basically, k훮k훮 were taking too long to turn towards bread as they walked towards it, resulting in them tracing very large, unrealistic arcs in their path towards the bread. By vastly increasing their turn rate (from 0.5 to 1.75), I was able to make them turn much more realistically-- and this had the added bonus of letting me cut out a lot of the error-catching chaff I was forced to implement when I had to compensate for a long, drawn-out turns, since now the k훮k훮 are almost guaranteed to be turned towards their target when the time comes to consume it. This in turn made stuff like eating the bread much less buggy-looking.
